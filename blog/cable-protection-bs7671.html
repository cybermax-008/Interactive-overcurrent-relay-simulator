Cable Protection with Overcurrent Relays: The BS 7671 Adiabatic Equation

How to verify that your relay clears faults fast enough to prevent cable insulation damage -- and what happens if it does not.

Why Cables Need Protection

When a short-circuit occurs in a power system, fault current flows through the conductors in the path between the source and the fault location. This current generates heat in the conductor according to the I-squared-R relationship. For the brief duration of a fault (typically a few hundred milliseconds to a few seconds), this heat has nowhere to go. The conductor and its insulation absorb all of it.

If the fault persists too long, the conductor temperature rises above the maximum permissible limit for its insulation material. For PVC-insulated cables, this limit is 160 degrees Celsius during a short circuit (up from a continuous rating of 70 degrees Celsius). For XLPE-insulated cables, the short-circuit limit is 250 degrees Celsius (up from 90 degrees Celsius continuous). Beyond these temperatures, the insulation degrades: PVC becomes brittle and cracks; XLPE softens, deforms, and may melt. In either case, the cable's dielectric integrity is compromised, potentially leading to further faults, fires, or complete cable failure.

The overcurrent relay protecting a cable section must therefore trip before the conductor reaches its limiting temperature. This is not optional -- it is a fundamental requirement of cable protection verified during every coordination study. The tool for this verification is the adiabatic equation.

The Adiabatic Equation

BS 7671 (the IET Wiring Regulations) and IEC 60364 provide a standard method for calculating the maximum time a cable can withstand a given fault current without exceeding its short-circuit temperature limit. The equation is:

t = ( k x S / I )2

Where:

  • t -- The maximum permissible fault duration in seconds. If the fault lasts longer than this, the cable insulation is damaged.
  • k -- A material constant that depends on the conductor material (copper or aluminium) and the insulation type (PVC, XLPE, EPR, mineral, etc.). This constant incorporates the specific heat capacity, density, and resistivity of the conductor along with the permitted temperature rise.
  • S -- The conductor cross-sectional area in square millimetres (mm^2). Larger conductors store more thermal energy and can withstand fault current for longer.
  • I -- The prospective fault current in amperes. Higher fault current generates more heat per unit time, reducing the permissible duration.

The equation is called "adiabatic" because it assumes that no heat is lost from the conductor during the fault -- all the I-squared-R energy goes into raising the conductor temperature. This assumption is valid for short fault durations (up to about 5 seconds). For longer durations, some heat does dissipate into the surrounding insulation and environment, making the adiabatic result conservative (the real permissible time is slightly longer than calculated). In protection engineering, conservative is good.

Rearranging the equation gives the minimum conductor size for a given fault current and clearance time: S = (I x sqrt(t)) / k. This form is used during cable sizing, while the original time-based form is used during protection coordination to verify that the relay trips fast enough.

Material Constants

The value of k depends on the conductor-insulation combination. BS 7671 Table 43.1 provides the standard values:

Conductor Insulation k Initial / Final Temp
Copper PVC (70C rated) 115 70C / 160C
Copper XLPE or EPR (90C rated) 143 90C / 250C
Aluminium PVC (70C rated) 76 70C / 160C
Aluminium XLPE or EPR (90C rated) 94 90C / 250C

Copper has a higher k than aluminium for the same insulation type because copper has a higher volumetric heat capacity -- it can absorb more energy per unit volume before reaching the same temperature. XLPE-insulated cables have a higher k than PVC-insulated cables because the permitted temperature rise is larger (250 minus 90 = 160 degrees for XLPE versus 160 minus 70 = 90 degrees for PVC), even though XLPE starts at a higher initial temperature.

The practical consequence is that XLPE cables are more tolerant of fault current than PVC cables of the same size and conductor material. A 95 mm^2 Cu/XLPE cable can withstand the same fault current for roughly 1.5 times longer than a 95 mm^2 Cu/PVC cable. This makes XLPE the preferred choice for installations where high fault levels are expected.

Reading the Cable Damage Curve on a TCC Chart

The adiabatic equation defines a relationship between current and time: t = (kS/I)^2. On a log-log time-current characteristic (TCC) chart, this plots as a straight line with a slope of -2. That is because taking the log of both sides gives log(t) = 2 x log(kS) - 2 x log(I), which is a line with slope -2 on the log(I) vs log(t) axes.

This line represents the cable's thermal damage boundary. Any point below and to the left of this line is safe -- the fault would be cleared before the cable is damaged. Any point above and to the right means the cable sustains thermal damage before the relay trips.

The protection requirement is straightforward: the relay's time-current curve must lie entirely below the cable damage line across the full range of fault currents that the cable might carry. If the relay curve crosses above the damage line at any current -- even at a single point -- there is a fault level at which the cable will be damaged before the relay clears the fault. This represents a protection gap that must be resolved by changing relay settings, changing the cable size, or adding additional protection.

On a typical TCC chart with multiple relay curves, the cable damage line runs from upper-left to lower-right as a steep diagonal. The Extremely Inverse relay curve (IEC EI, alpha = 2.0) also has a slope approaching -2 at high multiples, which is why EI curves are specifically recommended for cable protection -- they naturally follow the same I-squared-t characteristic as the cable damage limit.

A Worked Example

Consider a 95 mm^2 copper conductor with XLPE insulation, connected to the secondary side of a 10 MVA, 33/11 kV transformer with 8% impedance.

Step 1: Calculate the maximum fault current.

The secondary-side three-phase fault current at 100% is:

I_fault = (10 x 1000) / (11 x sqrt(3) x 0.08) = 6,561 A

Step 2: Calculate the cable damage time at maximum fault current.

Using the adiabatic equation with k = 143 (Cu/XLPE) and S = 95:

t = (143 x 95 / 6561)^2 = (13585 / 6561)^2 = (2.071)^2 = 4.29 seconds

The cable can withstand the maximum fault current for approximately 4.3 seconds before its insulation reaches 250 degrees Celsius.

Step 3: Check the relay operating time.

Suppose the protecting relay is set with CT primary = 600 A, pickup multiplier = 0.50 (I_set = 300 A), TMS = 0.20, and IEC Standard Inverse curve. The current multiple at maximum fault is:

M = 6561 / 300 = 21.87

The relay trip time is:

t = 0.14 / (21.87^0.02 - 1) x 0.20 = 0.14 / (1.0637 - 1) x 0.20 = 0.14 / 0.0637 x 0.20 = 0.44 seconds

The relay trips in 0.44 seconds, well within the cable's 4.29 second limit. The margin is 3.85 seconds -- more than adequate.

Step 4: Check at reduced fault levels.

At 20% fault level (1,312 A), the cable damage time is:

t = (13585 / 1312)^2 = (10.35)^2 = 107.2 seconds

And the relay trip time at M = 1312/300 = 4.37:

t = 0.14 / (4.37^0.02 - 1) x 0.20 = 0.14 / (1.0303 - 1) x 0.20 = 0.14 / 0.0303 x 0.20 = 0.92 seconds

Again, the relay (0.92 s) is far faster than the cable limit (107.2 s). Cable protection is satisfied across the range.

Tip: Cable protection is almost always satisfied at low fault levels because the cable damage time increases with the square of the current reduction, while the relay time increases more slowly. The critical check is at maximum fault current, where the cable damage time is shortest.

Using the Simulator's Cable Overlay

Rather than calculating cable damage times manually at each fault level, you can overlay the cable damage curve directly on the TCC chart in the simulator. This gives an immediate visual confirmation that the relay curve lies below the damage line everywhere.

To use the cable overlay feature:

  1. Open the simulator and configure your transformer and relay settings as normal.
  2. Locate the cable protection settings in the configuration panel.
  3. Select the conductor material (copper or aluminium) and insulation type (PVC or XLPE).
  4. Enter the conductor cross-sectional area in mm^2.
  5. The cable damage curve will appear on the TCC chart as a straight diagonal line. Visually confirm that all relay curves protecting this cable section lie below the damage line at every current level.

If any relay curve crosses above the cable damage line, you have several options: reduce the relay TMS for faster tripping, switch to an Extremely Inverse curve for a steeper characteristic that better matches the cable's I-squared-t limit, increase the cable size (larger S moves the damage line up), or add a separate high-set instantaneous element that provides fast clearance for close-up faults.

Key Takeaways

  • Cables have a finite thermal withstand time during faults. If the overcurrent relay does not clear the fault before this time expires, the cable insulation is permanently damaged.
  • The adiabatic equation t = (kS/I)^2 calculates the maximum permissible fault duration. It assumes no heat loss from the conductor, which is conservative and valid for durations up to about 5 seconds.
  • Material constants (k) depend on the conductor-insulation combination. Cu/XLPE (k=143) is the most tolerant; Al/PVC (k=76) is the least. Always use the correct k value for the installed cable type.
  • On a log-log TCC chart, the cable damage curve is a straight line with slope -2. The relay curve must lie entirely below this line. Extremely Inverse curves are the best match because they have a similar slope at high currents.
  • Always check cable protection at maximum fault current, where the damage time is shortest. Low fault levels are rarely a concern because the cable withstand time increases much faster than the relay operating time as current decreases.
